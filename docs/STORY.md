# Hollow Pines — The Search

*A short story, and the six who spend the night in it.*

## The Legend

Eleven hikers had vanished from Hollow Pines over forty years. Not all at once — that would have made
the news, made a task force, made a fence. They went one and two at a time, a decade apart, spaced just
widely enough that each disappearance belonged to its own year and its own small grief. A day-tripper in
'81. A father and son in '94 who left a cold campfire and a boot with the laces still tied. A pair of
graduate students in 2009 whose last GPS ping sat exactly on the old fire road and then simply stopped,
mid-stride, as if the map had ended there.

The official reports said *exposure*, *misadventure*, *lost*. The county had a template for it. The
unofficial reports — the ones traded on forums at two in the morning, the ones the rangers didn't file —
said something else. They said *tall*. They said *fast*. They said it stood at the edge of the firelight
and did not blink, and that the people who saw it and came back were the lucky ones, because they had
been *let* to come back.

There was one detail every account shared, believer and skeptic alike: it always happened on a quiet
night. Not stormy — quiet. One night a year the wind dropped, the owls shut up, the creek seemed to run
without sound, and the forest held its breath like a room that knows someone is about to walk into it.
The locals called it *the still night*. They stayed home for it. They had learned.

Dr. Mara Okonkwo did not believe in monsters. She believed in **data** — and in the maddening fact that
in a hundred years of looking, nobody had ever brought home a single frame that couldn't be laughed away.
Blurry. Shaky. Convenient. She intended to fix that. She spent her own grant money to put five people and
four hundred pounds of equipment at the edge of the Hollow Pines National Forest on the one night the
locals swore the woods went quiet, with one instruction that doubled as a prayer: *get it on camera, get
it clean, and get it home.*

The road in ends where the asphalt gives up and the gravel takes over, and the gravel gives up where the
trees close in. They left the van at the ranger trailhead with the last bar of cell signal and a sun
already going copper behind the ridgeline.

"Sundown in twenty minutes," said June, checking the sky the way only someone who has been genuinely lost
checks the sky — not for weather, for time she can't get back. "After that, the forest belongs to whatever
lives in it."

They split the gear. They split the dark. They turned on their flashlights.

Behind them, where the firelight didn't reach, the ferns moved against the wind — and went still.

## The Five Searchers

Each is a playable Searcher with a light asymmetric specialty. All of them can move, sprint, carry a
camera, revive a downed teammate, hold a light on the creature to dazzle it, and vault a fallen log.
Their specialty just makes them *better* at one thing — and each of them is here because of something
they can't put down.

### 🔬 Dr. Mara Okonkwo — *Lead Cryptozoologist*
Calm, exacting, the one who keeps the team from bolting when the trees start moving. Twenty years of her
career have been a slow, polite exile: every colleague who ever mattered to her stopped returning her
emails the week she published on Hollow Pines. This is not a hunt for her. It is an argument, and she
means to win it with evidence no committee can dismiss. **Specialty: Analysis.** She reads the trail —
which prints are fresh, which are a bear, which way the thing is actually moving — and keeps the team
from burning the night chasing elk tracks. *"We don't need to be brave. We need to be right, and on the
record. Stay together and keep filming."*

### 📷 Eli Vance — *Wildlife Photographer*
Has photographed grizzlies from six feet and lived to sell the prints. Believes, sincerely and against
all evidence, that the lens is braver than he is — that as long as he's looking through it, nothing can
quite reach him. **Specialty: The Photo.** His camera captures at longer range, and its flash can briefly
**stun and reveal** the creature. But a flash in that dark is a flare: it screams *here, right here* to
everything in the forest, and not everything in the forest is afraid of him. *"One clean frame. That's
all any of this has ever needed. One frame nobody can argue with."*

### 🥾 June "Junebug" Halloran — *Park Ranger & Tracker*
Knows these woods better than the survey maps do, because the maps were drawn in daylight. She lost a
friend to Hollow Pines fifteen years ago — a summer intern who walked off to check a trail marker and did
not come back — and she has never once said so out loud, not in the incident report, not to her husband,
not to the four people walking behind her right now. **Specialty: Tracking.** Fresh footprints and broken
brush glow faintly for her in the dark; she moves quiet, and she can mark the creature's trail for the
team. *"It's not lost out here. We are. Walk where I walk."*

### 🎙️ Theo Park — *Audio Engineer & Podcaster*
Came for a season finale — *the episode that finally breaks the show* — and is beginning to understand,
somewhere behind the microphone, that he is going to be *in* the story rather than telling it. He talks
when he's scared, which is most of tonight. **Specialty: Sound.** His parabolic mic picks up the
creature's movement and breathing from far off, buying the team the one thing the missing eleven never
had: *early warning.* *"Okay. Okay, that — everyone stop. That branch didn't break itself, and it's
closer than the last one."*

### 🩹 Sam Reyes — *Survivalist & Field Medic*
Ex–search-and-rescue, which means Sam has carried people out of these mountains before, and has also, once
or twice, carried out what was left when the search ran too long. Treats panic like a wound: name it,
dress it, keep moving. Is the only one who packed as if they might not all be walking back to the van.
**Specialty: Endurance.** Revives downed teammates faster, carries spare flashlight batteries to hand out,
and keeps a cooler head — more stamina — when the roaring starts. *"Nobody gets left in the dark. You go
down, I come get you. That's the whole deal. Say it back to me."*

## The Sixth Player — Bigfoot

Not a villain. A **resident**. Older than the trail signs, older than the fire road, older than the county
that keeps such careful files on it. It does not hate them; it does not think in the shape of hate. It
simply does not want to be *found*, and forty years of being looked for has taught it, with the patient
cruelty of something that has all the time in the world, exactly how the looking works.

It knows that light means people. It knows that people in a line means a search, and that a search is a
thing with a seam in it. It knows that if it makes a sound off to the left, the lights turn left, and one
of them always — *always* — drifts a little wide of the others to see. It knows that a person alone in the
dark stops being a searcher and becomes something simpler and quieter, and that the rest of the line does
not notice the gap until the gap is all there is.

It has watched a hundred years of people come apart in exactly this way. Tonight, for the first time, the
lights are trying something new. Tonight the lights are trying to stay *together*.

It is curious about that. It has all night to be curious.

## Three Nights

The match is three nights, each 8 p.m. to 8 a.m., and each one is a different animal.

**Night One — the probing.** It watches. It leaves prints where they'll be found, snaps a branch just past
the treeline, learns the team's shape: who leads, who lags, who turns at every sound and who doesn't turn
at all. The searchers learn too — how the dark sits, how far a flashlight really reaches, how much braver
five people are than one. Mara's voice on the radio at dusk: *"Slow is fine. Slow is data. We have three
nights."*

**Night Two — the pressure.** It's faster now, and it has stopped pretending it isn't there. The roars come
closer together. The prints run in straight lines instead of wandering. It has decided who the team can't
afford to lose, and it starts working to take that person into the dark for a while — not forever, just
long enough for the others to feel the gap. June, quieter than usual: *"It's not testing us anymore. It
knows what we do when we're scared. Don't do the thing it knows."*

**Night Three — the still night proper.** The wind is gone. The creek runs without sound. Whatever grace
the first two nights held has burned off, and both sides know the clock is the whole game now: three clean
videos before dawn, or none. It hunts like it means it. They film like they mean it. Theo, barely a
whisper, mic still running because he can't imagine turning it off now: *"Dawn's at six-fourteen. Forty
minutes. Forty minutes and we've either got it or we're a story someone tells on a forum at two in the
morning."*

## Dawn

If the searchers win, the footage airs. Three clean seconds, impossible to laugh away, and a forest that
will never again be just *exposure, misadventure, lost.* Mara gets her argument. The county opens the old
files. Somewhere, the families of eleven people finally get a word for what happened that isn't a shrug.

If Bigfoot wins, the sun comes up on a quiet camp and a story that stays a story. The team drives back to
the last bar of cell signal with cards full of shaky, convenient, arguable nothing, and the forest keeps
its secret the way it has kept every secret for forty years — completely, and without malice, and without
a trace.

Either way, dawn is at 6:14 a.m.

And either way, somebody out here has spent their last night looking for something that spent the whole
night looking back.
